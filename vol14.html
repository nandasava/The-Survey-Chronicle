Prologue — The Funeral

The church was packed. Not with mourners who loved him, but with those who had survived him. Jonasi Gomora lay in a polished mahogany casket, his face frozen in a grimace that seemed to mock the living. His four wives sat in the front row, each a statue of calculated grief. Joyce, the first wife, wore black lace and diamond earrings—the spoils of a war she had won. Matipa, the mistress who had clawed her way into the house, dabbed her eyes with a handkerchief that smelled of expensive perfume. Essie, the quiet second wife, stared blankly at the altar, her hands clasped so tightly her knuckles were white. Lindani, the youngest, sobbed openly—not because she had loved him, but because she had been his final mistake.

The pastor spoke of redemption, of a man who had given so much to his community. But no one in that room believed a word. They had all seen the truth—the infidelity, the manipulation, the cruelty that Jonasi had wrapped in a smile and a tailored suit. The service was a performance, a final act in a drama that had been playing out for decades.

As the pallbearers carried the casket out, Joyce allowed herself a small, private smile. She had waited twenty years for this moment. Twenty years of humiliation, of swallowing her pride, of building an empire only to watch him try to tear it down. And now, he was gone—not by accident, not by disease, but by her design.

The question that had haunted the series from the very first scene—who killed Jonasi Gomora?—was finally answered. But the answer was not a name. It was a story. And this is that story.

Part One — The Rise

Chapter 1: The Boy from Nothing

Jonasi Gomora was born in a shack in Soweto, the youngest of five children. His father was a drunk who beat his mother and disappeared before Jonasi could walk. His mother worked as a domestic cleaner, scrubbing floors for white families while dreaming of a better life for her son. From an early age, Jonasi learned two things: the world was brutal, and the only way out was to take what you wanted.

He was smart, charming, and ruthlessly ambitious. By the time he was twenty, he had dropped out of university and started a small transport business with a single beaten-up van. He worked eighteen-hour days, sleeping in the back of the van when he had to. His mother died of cancer in his second year of business, and he poured his grief into his work. Within a decade, he owned a fleet of trucks and a logistics empire that stretched across southern Africa.

But success was not enough. Jonasi craved validation—the kind that came from women. He had always been a serial womaniser, even in his lean years, but now his wealth gave him access to a different class of women. He treated them as trophies, objects to be collected and discarded. He never stayed with one woman for long—until he met Joyce.

Chapter 2: Joyce — The First Wife

Joyce Ndlovu was a university student when she met Jonasi at a business networking event. She was sharp, ambitious, and from a respectable family. Jonasi was immediately captivated—not just by her beauty, but by her intelligence. She was the first woman who challenged him, who saw through his charm and demanded respect. He pursued her relentlessly, and after a year of courtship, she agreed to marry him.

Their wedding was modest—Jonasi was still building his empire—but Joyce was determined to be the perfect wife. She helped him with his business, managed his household, and gave him three children: Mpume, Menzi, and a younger daughter. For the first decade of their marriage, they were a formidable team. Jonasi's business flourished, and Joyce became a social media influencer, curating an image of marital bliss that made her a local celebrity.

But behind closed doors, Jonasi's old habits never died. He had affairs—brief, secret encounters that he thought Joyce would never discover. He convinced himself that these were harmless, that they didn't affect his marriage. But each betrayal chipped away at Joyce's trust. She began to suspect, but she had invested too much—her reputation, her family, her identity—to walk away. So she stayed, and she built walls around her heart.

Chapter 3: The Other Women

Jonasi's infidelities were not casual. They were calculated, strategic. He saw women as assets, as ways to expand his influence and satisfy his ego. The first to break through the walls of his marriage was Matipa Nkosi, a young employee at J&J Holdings. She was ambitious, seductive, and refused to be a secret. She wanted a title, a ring, a seat at the table. Jonasi, flattered by her boldness, gave her a position and, eventually, a place in his bed.

Then came Essie, a quiet woman from a traditional family. She was different from Matipa—submissive, loyal, content to be a second wife as long as she had some part of him. Jonasi married her in a private ceremony, hidden from Joyce. Essie was his secret, his escape from the demands of his public life. She accepted his absences, his lies, his broken promises, because she believed that one day he would choose her.

And finally, there was Lindani—a young woman who saw Jonasi as her ticket out of poverty. She was the most vulnerable, the most naïve. She believed his promises of a future together, unaware of the web of deceit that surrounded her. Jonasi took her, used her, and discarded her when she became inconvenient. But Lindani would not disappear quietly. She would have her revenge, though not in the way anyone expected.

Part Two — The Collapse

Chapter 4: The Anniversary That Broke Everything

On the eve of their twentieth wedding anniversary, Joyce discovered the divorce papers. Jonasi had filed without telling her, planning to discard her like a worn-out business asset. The papers were delivered to their home in a plain envelope, and Joyce opened them with trembling hands. She read the cold legal language—"irretrievable breakdown of the marriage"—and felt the floor drop from under her.

She confronted Jonasi that night. He was cold, dismissive. He told her that their marriage was over, that he had found someone else, that she should sign the papers and move on. Joyce refused. She had given him everything—her youth, her career, her dignity—and she would not be cast aside like a discarded mistress. She staged a glamorous anniversary party, inviting all their friends and business associates, forcing Jonasi to smile and play the role of the devoted husband while she seethed with rage.

The party was a masterpiece of passive aggression. Joyce made a toast to their twenty years, her voice dripping with sarcasm. Jonasi sat beside her, his jaw tight, his eyes burning with hatred. The guests, oblivious to the tension, congratulated them on their perfect marriage. It was the last time they would appear in public together as a united front.

Chapter 5: The Secret First Love

As Joyce dug deeper into Jonasi's past, she uncovered a secret that shook her to her core. Before Joyce, before the empire, Jonasi had a first love—a woman named Nomsa. She was the only woman he had ever truly loved, the one he had been forced to leave behind when his business took off. He had never told Joyce about her, but Nomsa had never left his heart. He had been visiting her for years, maintaining a parallel life that Joyce never suspected.

The revelation broke something inside Joyce. She realised that she had never been his first choice, that she had been a consolation prize. The fury that had been simmering for years finally boiled over. She began to plot her revenge, not with tears or accusations, but with cold, calculated precision. She would destroy Jonasi in a way that would leave him broken, humiliated, and utterly alone.

Chapter 6: The Disease

Jonasi's recklessness finally caught up with him. His overlapping relationships led to a gonorrhoea outbreak that spread through the entire household. Joyce, Matipa, Essie, and Lindani all contracted the disease. The news came as a devastating blow, exposing the full extent of Jonasi's betrayal. The wives, once rivals, now shared a common enemy. They formed an uneasy alliance, united in their hatred of the man who had destroyed their lives.

Jonasi, for the first time, showed vulnerability. He begged for forgiveness, promised to change, tried to buy back their loyalty with gifts and apologies. But the damage was done. The women had seen the truth—Jonasi was not a man who could be trusted. He was a narcissist who would never put anyone above his own desires.

Part Three — The Revenge

Chapter 7: Joyce's Masterplan

Joyce knew that Jonasi would never change. He would continue to lie, cheat, and manipulate as long as he had the power to do so. The only way to stop him was to take that power away. She began to plot a revenge that was as meticulous as it was ruthless.

She hired an HIV-positive woman to seduce Jonasi. The woman was a ghost—no connection to Joyce, no trail to follow. She met Jonasi at a bar, played on his ego, and made him feel like a king. He fell for it, as Joyce knew he would. Within weeks, he had contracted HIV from the woman. When he discovered the diagnosis, he was shattered. He refused treatment, convinced that he could fight the disease with willpower alone. Joyce watched his decline with cold satisfaction.

But she wasn't finished. She took Jonasi in during his final days, playing the role of the devoted wife. She manipulated Menzi, their son, into sweet-talking his father into signing over most of his assets. Menzi, who had always craved his father's approval, willingly did as his mother asked. Jonasi, weak and desperate for affection, signed the documents with trembling hands. He died shortly after, believing that he had made peace with his family.

Chapter 8: The Final Twist

The last shot of the series was not of Jonasi's casket, but of Menzi in bed with Lindani—the woman who had once been his father's lover. In trying to get revenge on her husband, Joyce had never noticed her own son treading the same destructive path. Menzi had become his father's true successor, not in business, but in betrayal. The cycle of infidelity and heartbreak continued, unbroken.

Joyce, having won her war, lost the peace. Her son was now the new patriarch, and he was already repeating his father's mistakes. The empire she had helped build would pass to a man who would never be faithful, never be satisfied, never be whole.

Epilogue — The Legacy

The Polygamist is not a story about a man who died. It is a story about the people he left behind—the wives he betrayed, the children he neglected, and the lovers he used. It is a story about how one man's greed and lust can poison an entire family, and how the victims of that poison can become its perpetrators.

Joyce Gomora, once the picture of wifely devotion, became a cold and calculating avenger. She had started the series as a victim, but she ended it as a victor—though the victory was hollow. Matipa, who had fought so hard to be the wife, ended up with nothing but bitter memories. Essie, the quiet second wife, retreated into silence, haunted by the love that had never been returned. Lindani, the youngest, was left to pick up the pieces of a life that had been shattered.

And Menzi, the son who had inherited his father's worst traits, was now the new face of the Gomora dynasty—a dynasty built on lies, secrets, and broken promises.

The Polygamist is a cautionary tale, a mirror held up to a society where polygamy is often romanticised but rarely examined for its toxic consequences. It asks the question: can a man truly love multiple women, or is polygamy simply an institutionalised form of infidelity? The answer, as the series shows, is not simple. But the consequences are always the same.

Jonasi Gomora died alone, not because of a disease, but because he had never truly loved anyone—not even himself. His wives had survived him, but they had also been changed by him. They would carry the scars of his betrayals forever.

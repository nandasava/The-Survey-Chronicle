01 — The Anatomy of Fake Survey Responses

The Four Types of Bad Data Quietly Destroying Your Research

Not all bad responses look the same. Understanding the taxonomy of survey fraud is the first step to preventing it — and to choosing a platform that eliminates it before it reaches you.

🤖

Bot Responses

Automated scripts that complete surveys in under 10 seconds, selecting random options or always choosing the same position. They inflate your response count while producing meaningless data. Prevalent on any survey with a public link and financial incentive.

👤

Duplicate Humans

Real people filling in your survey multiple times — from the same IP, with slightly varied demographic details, to collect multiple incentive payments. Without IP deduplication and cookie tracking, you have no way of detecting them.

😴

Satisficers

Real people, genuinely completing your survey — but with minimum effort. They straight-line matrix questions (selecting the same column every time), click through without reading, and choose the first visible option on every question. They look like valid data. They are not.

🎭

Misrepresented Demographics

Respondents who lie about their age, location, income, or professional background to qualify for surveys they shouldn't be in. Without identity verification, you have no mechanism to screen them. Your "18–24 year-old UK professionals" sample may contain 50-year-olds in other countries.

04 — The Satisficing Problem

Survey Satisficing: The Silent Killer of Research Validity

Satisficing is when respondents give "good enough" answers rather than thoughtful ones. They're real people. They're completing your survey. The data looks normal. But it's garbage.

Signs of satisficing in your data

Identical response patterns across long matrix sections. Unusually fast completion times (under 90 seconds for a 10-minute survey). All responses clustered at the scale midpoint. Contradictory answers on similar questions. Nonsensical open-text responses.

How to prevent it in design

Limit matrix questions to 5–7 rows before breaking. Randomise question and scale order. Include reverse-coded items. Use forced-choice formats where appropriate. Add attention checks that flag disengaged respondents automatically.

"Attention checks should not feel like trick questions. They should be effortless for engaged respondents and impossible for satisficers — a canary in the coal mine, not a trap."

— Survey design best practice, 2026

How to Write an Attention Check Question

An effective attention check is explicit: "We want to make sure our survey is working correctly. Please select 'Strongly Agree' for this question." Any respondent who doesn't follow this instruction is flagged for removal. Simple. Powerful. SentinelSphere-AI's free plan supports attention check logic natively.

Check completion times against your expected survey duration — responses under 30% of median time are suspect. Run IP deduplication to catch duplicate submissions from the same address. What is survey satisficing and how do I prevent it?
Survey satisficing is when respondents complete a survey with minimum cognitive effort — straight-lining matrix questions, selecting the first option on every question, or rushing through without reading. Prevent it by limiting matrix questions to 5–7 rows, randomising question and answer order, including attention check items, using forced-choice formats selectively, and sourcing participants from incentivised verified panels where quality engagement is expected. Use IP address deduplication to block multiple submissions from the same network address. Enable browser cookie prevention to block re-submissions from the same browser. What is the difference between anonymous and confidential surveys?
Anonymous surveys collect no identifying information whatsoever — the researcher cannot link any response to any individual. Confidential surveys collect identity but the researcher commits not to disclose it publicly. Anonymous surveys typically achieve higher response rates and more honest answers, particularly for sensitive topics. Confidential surveys allow follow-up contact and longitudinal tracking. For IRB-compliant research, be precise about which your study uses — conflating the two is a common and serious methodological error.
How do I calculate margin of error from my survey data?
Margin of error (MOE) = Z × √(p(1–p)/n), where Z is your confidence level z-score (1.96 for 95%), p is the proportion (use 0.5 for maximum conservatism), and n is your sample size. For a sample of 385, this gives ±5% at 95% confidence. For 1,067, it gives ±3%. Always report margin of error alongside your findings — a result without it is statistically meaningless. What survey question types are best for measuring attitudes and opinions?
Likert scales (5 or 7 points) are the standard for measuring agreement or frequency of attitudes — widely used, easy to analyse statistically, and suitable for mean comparison across groups. Semantic differential scales measure bipolar constructs (e.g., "weak–strong", "negative–positive") and work well for brand perception. Matrix questions efficiently collect multiple Likert ratings but increase satisficing risk when overused. Net Promoter Score (NPS) measures recommendation intent on a 0–10 scale. How do I handle missing data in survey responses?
First, distinguish between missing completely at random (MCAR), missing at random (MAR), and missing not at random (MNAR) — each requires a different treatment. For small amounts of MCAR data (under 5%), listwise deletion (removing incomplete cases) is acceptable. For larger missing data, consider multiple imputation or pairwise deletion depending on your analysis. Prevent missing data at design stage by minimising required questions, saving partial responses (SentinelSphere-AI supports this), and reducing survey length to improve completion rates.
